# The real purpose of the Request for Proposal process

A Request for Proposal is a formal document that an organization sends to selected vendors asking them to propose a solution for a business problem. In Acme scenario, the real problem is not “move to cloud.” The real problem is something like this:

> Information technology is fragmented
> Delivery is slow
> Provisioning takes too long
> Shadow information technology exists
> Cost is not well controlled
> Accountability is weak
> Application and infrastructure standards are inconsistent
> Business teams are not getting what they need fast enough

So the Request for Proposal is not just a buying exercise. It is a structured process for turning a messy business problem into a clear market ask. That means the process starts long before the document is sent out.

*The full process from inception to release*

# Stage 1: Trigger and Problem Recognition

## 📌 What this Stage Is

This is the point where **Acme realizes that the current operating model is no longer sustainable**.

At this stage, the realization is often informal and driven by leadership sentiment rather than structured analysis.

> “This is not sustainable. We need to change how technology is delivered.”

---

## 🚨 Common Triggers

The realization is typically driven by one or more of the following issues:

| Category | Trigger |
|--------|--------|
| 💰 Cost | High or unpredictable IT costs |
| ⚙️ Operations | Slow provisioning |
| 🚫 Reliability | Repeated outages |
| 📉 Quality | Poor service quality |
| 🧾 Compliance | Failed audits |
| 📢 Business Feedback | Frequent complaints from business teams |
| 🚀 Innovation | Inability to launch products quickly |
| 🤖 Automation | Low automation levels |
| 👁️ Visibility | Poor visibility into assets and costs |
| 🛑 Governance | Too many manual approvals |
| 👤 Dependency | Reliance on a few key individuals |
| 🌍 Consistency | Inconsistent operations across regions or business units |

---

## 👥 Stakeholders Involved (Customer Side)

Multiple stakeholders contribute to recognizing and framing the problem:

| Role | Responsibility / Perspective |
|------|----------------------------|
| 🧑‍💼 Chief Information Officer (CIO) | Owns IT strategy and operating model |
| 🧑‍💻 Chief Technology Officer (CTO) | Drives future-state technology vision (if role is separate) |
| 💰 Chief Financial Officer (CFO) / Finance | Focuses on cost inefficiencies and unpredictability |
| 📊 Business Unit Leaders | Concerned about delays impacting revenue and customer experience |
| 🏗️ Head of Infrastructure / Cloud / Operations | Provides insight into operational challenges |
| 🧠 Enterprise Architecture Team | Frames the problem structurally and strategically |

---

## 📄 Outputs of This Stage

This stage typically does **not produce formal documentation**.

Instead, it results in early signals such as:

| Output Type | Description |
|------------|------------|
| ⚠️ Executive Concern | Leadership acknowledges issues |
| 🎯 Transformation Intent | Agreement that change is required |
| 🔍 Exploration Decision | Decision to investigate solutions |

---

## ⚠️ What Can Go Wrong

A weak problem definition can lead to **misguided transformation efforts**.

### ❌ Narrow Framing (Common Mistake)

> “We need cloud”

### ✅ Correct Framing (Strategic View)

> “We need a new operating model covering:
> - Infrastructure
> - Applications
> - Governance
> - Automation
> - Service delivery”

---

## 🧠 Key Insight

> **This stage is not about choosing solutions.  
It is about correctly defining the problem.**

If the problem is framed incorrectly, the entire transformation will be misaligned.

---

## 🔗 Summary

| Element | Key Takeaway |
|--------|-------------|
| Stage Goal | Recognize that current model is unsustainable |
| Nature | Informal, leadership-driven |
| Output | Intent, not documentation |
| Risk | Poor problem framing |
| Success Factor | Broad, strategic understanding of the problem |
